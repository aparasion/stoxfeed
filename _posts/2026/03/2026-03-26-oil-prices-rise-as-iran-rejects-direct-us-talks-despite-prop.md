Oil prices surged on Thursday as Iran dismissed the possibility of direct negotiations with the U.S., despite ongoing discussions regarding a ceasefire proposal. Brent crude futures increased by 1.21% to $103.46 per barrel, while West Texas Intermediate futures rose 1.35% to $91.54 per barrel. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi emphasized that mediated exchanges do not equate to negotiations, further complicating the geopolitical landscape.

This development is significant for financial markets, particularly in the energy sector, as rising oil prices can influence inflation expectations and consumer spending. Analysts at TD Securities suggest that while the current oil shock is notable, it is unlikely to provoke a swift policy response from the Federal Reserve. They anticipate the Fed will maintain a cautious stance, potentially delaying rate hikes, as long as inflation expectations remain stable.

Market professionals should consider the implications of sustained elevated oil prices on inflation and Fed policy, as these factors could shape investment strategies in the near term.
