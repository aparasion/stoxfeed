The ongoing geopolitical tensions, particularly the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, have led to unprecedented disruptions in global oil and LNG supplies, pushing prices above $100 per barrel and doubling natural gas prices in Europe. This critical chokepoint, responsible for 20% of daily global trade, has been largely inaccessible for over three weeks, highlighting the vulnerability of energy markets and the interconnectedness of global supply chains. As Asian refiners face soaring premiums for non-Middle Eastern crude, many are adjusting processing rates, while Europe grapples with an LNG supply crunch exacerbated by intensified competition from Asia.

The implications extend beyond immediate price spikes; they underscore the strategic importance of resource control in modern economies and military capabilities. The U.S. and its allies are racing to reduce reliance on Chinese rare earths and critical minerals, which are vital for defense and technology sectors. Initiatives like the U.S. Strategic Critical Minerals Reserve aim to mitigate this dependence, but significant supply shortfalls are anticipated due to lengthy lead times for new mining operations.

As the global landscape shifts, market professionals should closely monitor developments in energy and critical minerals, as control over these resources will increasingly dictate economic stability and strategic power.
