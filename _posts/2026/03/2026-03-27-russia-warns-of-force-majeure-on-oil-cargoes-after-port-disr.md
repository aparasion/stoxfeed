Russian oil exporters are facing significant disruptions, with key Baltic ports like Ust-Luga halting operations due to Ukrainian drone strikes on critical infrastructure. The situation has escalated to the point where producers are considering declaring force majeure on shipments, potentially impacting up to 40% of Russia’s seaborne crude and product flows. While Primorsk has partially resumed loadings, the overall capacity remains severely constrained, with full recovery not expected until mid-April.

Despite these operational challenges, Russia is experiencing a surge in oil revenues, driven by high global prices, with Brent crude surpassing $100 per barrel. This financial windfall has led the Kremlin to reconsider budget priorities, including military spending, even as the risk of prolonged export disruptions looms. The ongoing conflict and targeted strikes on export capacity signal that the situation may worsen, tightening the global oil supply further.

Market professionals should monitor the evolving dynamics in Russian oil exports, as sustained outages could exacerbate existing supply constraints and influence global pricing trends.
