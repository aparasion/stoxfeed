Bitcoin rebounded to $67,400 after a brief dip below $65,200, coinciding with escalating tensions in the Iran conflict, which has now entered its fifth week. The conflict's expansion, marked by Houthi involvement and U.S. troop deployments, sent Bitcoin to a low of $65,112—its lowest point since the February crash—before buyers stepped in as Asian markets opened. This volatility underscores the cryptocurrency's sensitivity to geopolitical events and market sentiment.

The recent price action raises concerns about Bitcoin's ability to maintain its pattern of higher lows, especially as Brent crude oil surged to around $115 per barrel, reflecting broader inflationary pressures. The potential for delayed Federal Reserve rate cuts, driven by rising oil and industrial metal prices, complicates the macroeconomic landscape. While Bitcoin has shown resilience in recovering from its lows, the shift in trading patterns could signal a pivotal moment for the asset.

Market participants should closely monitor Bitcoin's performance around the $65,000 level, as a sustained break below this range could indicate a shift in investor sentiment and broader implications for risk assets amid ongoing geopolitical uncertainties.
