The potential closure of the Strait of Hormuz, a critical artery for global energy supply, poses a significant threat to the oil and natural gas markets. This narrow passage, through which approximately 20 million barrels of oil and 2 million barrels of liquefied natural gas transit daily, could leave a supply gap exceeding 10 million barrels per day if disrupted for an extended period. Governments and energy markets are now grappling with how to mitigate this risk, with options such as strategic petroleum reserve releases and alternative pipeline routes from Saudi Arabia and the UAE offering only partial relief.

The implications for the financial markets are profound. A sustained disruption could lead to skyrocketing energy prices, increased competition for available cargoes, and potential curtailment of industrial operations in regions heavily reliant on natural gas. Historical precedents indicate that such supply shocks can have lasting effects, potentially rippling through to agricultural sectors and consumer prices.
