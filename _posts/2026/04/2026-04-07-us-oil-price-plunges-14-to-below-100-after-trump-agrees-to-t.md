U.S. crude oil prices experienced a sharp decline on Tuesday evening, dropping over 14% to $96.21 per barrel, following President Trump's announcement of a two-week ceasefire with Iran. This decision hinges on Iran's agreement to fully reopen the Strait of Hormuz, a critical passage for global oil supplies. The ceasefire comes after a tense escalation in rhetoric, with Trump previously threatening severe military action if Iran did not comply with U.S. demands.

The implications for the financial markets are significant. The Strait of Hormuz is vital, as it facilitates approximately 20% of the world's oil exports. Disruptions in this region have already led to soaring prices for crude oil, jet fuel, and diesel, with analysts warning of potential global fuel shortages if the situation does not stabilize. The market's reaction reflects heightened sensitivity to geopolitical tensions affecting supply chains.

For market professionals, the key takeaway is the importance of monitoring developments in U.S.-Iran relations and their potential impact on oil prices and broader energy markets. A successful negotiation could alleviate price pressures, while continued instability could exacerbate supply concerns.
