The dollar index reached a 1.5-week high on Thursday, closing up 0.22%, driven by heightened safe-haven demand amid ongoing Middle East tensions and rising crude oil prices. These factors are increasing inflation expectations, which could influence the Federal Reserve's policy decisions. While the April S&P manufacturing PMI showed robust growth, a rise in weekly jobless claims and a decline in the Chicago Fed National Activity Index tempered dollar gains.

The dollar's strength negatively impacted the euro, which fell to a 1.5-week low, as surging oil prices pose challenges for the Eurozone economy. Mixed economic signals from Europe, including a strong manufacturing PMI but a contracting services sector, suggest a complex outlook for the euro. Meanwhile, the yen also weakened against the dollar, influenced by rising energy costs and mixed economic data from Japan.

Market participants should note that the dollar's recent performance, bolstered by geopolitical tensions and economic data, may lead to shifts in interest rate expectations, particularly as the Fed's next meeting approaches. This dynamic could create further volatility in currency pairs and impact commodity prices.
