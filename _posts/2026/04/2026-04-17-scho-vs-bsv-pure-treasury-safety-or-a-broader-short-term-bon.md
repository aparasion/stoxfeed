The Schwab Short-Term U.S. Treasury ETF (SCHO) and the Vanguard Short-Term Bond ETF (BSV) are gaining attention for their low expense ratios and similar yields, but they cater to different risk profiles. Both ETFs target conservative investors seeking modest returns with limited volatility, yet their portfolio compositions diverge significantly. SCHO focuses almost exclusively on U.S. Treasury securities, providing a risk-free investment option, while BSV includes a mix of government and investment-grade corporate bonds, introducing a slight credit risk.

This distinction matters as it affects performance and risk exposure in varying economic conditions. While both funds charge an identical 0.03% expense ratio, SCHO's ultra-conservative strategy may appeal to those prioritizing safety, especially in uncertain markets. In contrast, BSV offers a broader exposure that could yield higher returns during stable economic periods but may introduce volatility during downturns.

For investors, the key takeaway is to assess their risk tolerance: SCHO offers pure Treasury safety, while BSV provides a more diversified approach to short-term bond investing.
