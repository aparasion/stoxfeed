Oil markets are experiencing extreme volatility following the outbreak of conflict in the Middle East, with Brent crude prices soaring over 55% since late February, peaking near $120 per barrel. The escalation began with U.S.-Israeli strikes against Iran, which prompted Tehran to retaliate and disrupt energy exports across the Gulf, leading to significant price fluctuations. March saw one of the largest monthly surges in oil prices on record, driven by fears of supply disruptions through the critical Strait of Hormuz.

The ongoing conflict has major implications for energy markets, as production cuts from Gulf nations and attacks on oil infrastructure have compounded supply concerns. Analysts suggest that even if negotiations lead to a ceasefire, the market will remain tight due to infrastructure damage and ongoing geopolitical tensions. Commodity Context founder Rory Johnston highlighted that any potential reopening of the Strait could trigger a temporary price drop, but sustained supply chain issues may keep Brent prices anchored between $80 and $90.

Market professionals should closely monitor developments in U.S.-Iran negotiations and regional stability, as these factors will heavily influence oil price trajectories and overall market sentiment in the coming weeks.
