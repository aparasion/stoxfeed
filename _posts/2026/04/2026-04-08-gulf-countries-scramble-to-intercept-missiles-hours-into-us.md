Tensions in the Middle East escalated as several countries reported missile and drone attacks from Iran on Wednesday, despite a newly announced two-week ceasefire between the U.S. and Iran. This ceasefire, brokered by Pakistan, aims to facilitate negotiations in Islamabad, but the ongoing hostilities raise doubts about its viability. Iranian officials indicated that their military operations would cease only if attacks against Iran stop, suggesting potential ambiguities in compliance.

The implications for financial markets are significant, particularly for energy prices and regional stability. The continued missile threats and the depletion of interceptor stocks among Gulf states could lead to heightened volatility in oil markets, especially given that recent strikes have already disrupted energy infrastructure, including a major LNG facility in Qatar. With the Gulf region's security dynamics in flux, investors should monitor developments closely.

One key takeaway is that while the ceasefire offers a temporary reprieve, the persistent military actions and strained defenses in the Gulf highlight the fragility of regional stability, which could impact energy supply and pricing in the near term.
