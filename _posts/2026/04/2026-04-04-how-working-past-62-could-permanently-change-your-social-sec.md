The decision to claim Social Security retirement benefits at age 62 can significantly impact long-term financial security, as highlighted by the Federal Reserve's findings that the median U.S. household has only $185,000 saved for retirement. While many are tempted to claim benefits early, doing so can reduce monthly payouts for life, with penalties of up to 30% for those who take benefits before reaching full retirement age, which is 67 for those born in 1960 or later.

Working beyond age 62 can enhance Social Security benefits in three key ways: it allows individuals to replace low-earning years in their benefit calculation, increases monthly payouts if claimed at full retirement age, and provides delayed retirement credits that can boost benefits by 24% for each year benefits are delayed past age 67. This could mean an increase of over $1,000 in monthly benefits by waiting until age 70.

For financial professionals, understanding these nuances is crucial for advising clients on retirement planning. Timing the claim can lead to substantial differences in retirement income, making it essential to consider individual circumstances and consult with financial experts.
